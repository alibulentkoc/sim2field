Full derivation - the cycle-time budget

Why time is the binding constraint

The whole pick cycle must finish before the next fruit arrives.
\[ T_{\mathrm{arrival}} = \frac{d}{v} \qquad T_{\mathrm{cycle}} \le T_{\mathrm{arrival}} \]

The arrival interval \(T_{\mathrm{arrival}} = d/v\) is the time between fruit reaching the station. The whole pick cycle must finish inside it, or the machine falls behind and fruit passes unpicked.

Consequence

Time is the binding constraint that ties the spine together - one number every subsystem is measured against.

Sum the mission-FSM stage times to size the cycle.
\[ T_{\mathrm{cycle}} = 0.3 + 0.2 + 1.0 + 1.0 + 0.8 + 1.5 + 0.8 + 1.0 = 6.6\ \mathrm{s} \]

Adding the stage times (illustrative, Module 1 Sec. 7.1): DRIVE 0.3 + STAGE 0.2 + ALIGN 1.0 + GRAB 1.0 + LIFT 0.8 + SWING 1.5 + LOWER 0.8 + RELEASE 1.0. The dominant stage is SWING at 1.5 s - the transfer swing that moves the fruit from vine to bin.

Consequence

The sum reveals feasibility and names the stage to attack first (EI-05): SWING carries the most time.

The constraint bites: the cycle time caps the rover speed.
\[ v \le \frac{d}{T_{\mathrm{cycle}}} = \frac{1.2}{6.6} = 0.18\ \mathrm{m/s} \]

The ratified target is \(v \le 0.20\ \mathrm{m/s}\) at \(T_{\mathrm{cycle}} \le 6.0\ \mathrm{s}\) (Doc B). The naive single-arm cycle (6.6 s) forces the rover below the target - the budget did not describe a design, it constrained one and exposed a tension.

Consequence

The levers: attack the dominant stage (SWING), or change the architecture (a second arm, buffering, a faster transfer). Modules 8-10 and the capstone exist to resolve exactly this.

The concept - a budget constrains, it does not describe

What a systems budget is for

CEC-01 - The Signal-to-Action Spine

The harvester is one machine that turns a camera frame into a grasped fruit; it decomposes by function into Perceive, Decide, Act - and every subsystem exists because the spine requires it. The cycle-time budget is what makes the spine verifiable: it converts a qualitative loop into a per-stage time allocation, and the moment you sum the stages it exposes whether the architecture can meet the mission. Here it forced a choice - a faster transfer or a second arm - long before any part was built. That is what a systems budget is for: not to describe a design, but to constrain one and reveal its tensions early, when they are cheap to fix.

Engineering takeaway - what to do next

Where the next effort belongs

  • -> Build the budget before the mechanism. Summing the stage times tells you whether the architecture can meet the mission - and which stage to fix - before any hardware is committed.
  • -> The single-arm cycle is dominated by SWING (transfer). The first move is a faster transfer path; if that is not enough, a second arm parallelizes the pick and roughly doubles throughput. Attack the dominant stage before adding complexity.
  • -> Speed and throughput are the same budget seen two ways: \(v = d / T_{\mathrm{cycle}}\). Cutting the cycle both raises the safe rover speed and the fruit-per-hour - confirm the faster speed still respects the placement budget (CEC-03) and the edge latency (CEC-04).
  • -> Decision: treat the cycle-time budget as the integrating contract for the whole spine - it is where perception, reach, grip, and mobility are reconciled against one mission number. Resolve its tension by architecture first, tuning second.
