Worked Example / Module 5 / CEC-03 / The Placement Error Budget

Every source of error gets one shared budget.

Perception, calibration, estimation, and mechanism each contribute error. This is the contract that binds them: their combined 3-sigma spread must fit inside the gripper's capture tolerance - or the fruit falls outside the jaws. Here it is, worked end to end, then made live.

Governing relation
\[ 3\,\sigma_{\mathrm{place}} \;=\; 3\sqrt{\sum_{i}\sigma_i^{2}} \;\le\; c \]
The 3-sigma placement spread must fit within the gripper capture tolerance c.
Full derivation - from covariance to a single gate

How the budget is built

Each stage delivers a pose with uncertainty, not a bare point.
\[ \Sigma_y \;=\; J\,\Sigma_x\,J^{\mathsf{T}} \]

For each stage \(y = f(x)\), the covariance transforms through the stage Jacobian \(J\). Chaining the stages accumulates the Jacobians into one fruit-pose covariance in the robot frame. Along the sensitive placement axis, that covariance reduces to a set of independent one-dimensional contributors \(\sigma_i\).

Consequence

The pose is delivered with a covariance, not as a bare point (SR-F-08) - uncertainty is a first-class output of perception.

Independent errors combine as a root-sum-square.
\[ \sum_i \sigma_i^{2} \;=\; 8^2+3^2+3^2+4^2+4^2+6^2 \;=\; 150\ \mathrm{mm^2} \]

The six ratified contributors along the placement axis (Doc B Sec. 5.2): depth \(\sigma_Z=8\) (dominant, at the staged pick range), calibration \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cal}}=3\), hand-eye \(\sigma_{\mathrm{he}}=3\), timing \(\sigma_{\mathrm{sync}}=4\), estimation \(\sigma_{\mathrm{est}}=4\), and mechanism \(\sigma_{\mathrm{mech}}=6\) (allocated to M8). Because they are independent, it is the variances that add - not the standard deviations.

Consequence

The depth term enters as \(8^2=64\) - already 43% of the whole budget before any other source is counted.

Take the root, scale to 3-sigma, and test the gate.
\[ \sigma_{\mathrm{place}} = \sqrt{150} = 12.25\ \mathrm{mm} \qquad 3\,\sigma_{\mathrm{place}} = 36.74\ \mathrm{mm} \]

The 3-sigma spread is the radius that contains the placement error with high confidence. The design passes when it fits inside the capture tolerance \(c = 40\ \mathrm{mm}\):

\[ 3\,\sigma_{\mathrm{place}} = 36.74\ \mathrm{mm} \;\le\; c = 40\ \mathrm{mm} \quad\Rightarrow\quad \textsf{PASS} \]
Consequence

It passes - but with only 3.26 mm of headroom. That thin margin is the reason the rest of this example matters: where should the next millimetre come from?

The budget, live - shrink a term and watch the total

Engineering Insight EI-05 / the numeric proof

Attack the dominant term

From the ratified baseline, halve one contributor and measure the payoff. The math is unforgiving in a useful way - and it decides which subsystem is worth improving.

Halve the dominant term \(\sigma_Z:\ 8 \to 4\)

30.30 mm
\[ 3\sqrt{4^2+3^2+3^2+4^2+4^2+6^2}=3\sqrt{102} \]

Halve a small term \(\sigma_{\mathrm{cal}}:\ 3 \to 1.5\)

35.91 mm
\[ 3\sqrt{8^2+1.5^2+3^2+4^2+4^2+6^2}=3\sqrt{143.25} \]
EI-05 - Attack the Dominant Error Source

In a root-sum-square budget, the largest term dominates the total - find it and reduce it first. Halving the dominant depth term buys roughly eight times the improvement of halving a small calibration term, for comparable effort. This is precisely why the architecture stages the fruit close (depth error grows as \(Z^{2}\)) instead of chasing calibration to the third decimal. Budget first, find the dominant contributor, spend effort there - then repeat until no single term dominates.

Engineering takeaway / what to do next

Where the next effort belongs

  • -> The budget passes, but with only 3.3 mm of margin - not comfortable. It is worth improving before the pilot, where real-world terms tend to grow.
  • -> Improving calibration by another millimetre moves \(3\sigma_{\mathrm{place}}\) by well under a millimetre. That effort is almost wasted here.
  • -> Improving stereo depth accuracy - or reducing the working distance so depth error (\(\sim Z^{2}\)) shrinks - produces by far the largest improvement per unit effort.
  • -> Decision: the next engineering effort belongs in perception geometry - the sensor and the staging distance - not in calibration. Re-run the budget after, and re-check which term dominates next.
