Worked Example / Module 8 / EI-08 / Kinematics & Singularities

Reach every melon - and never through a singularity.

The manipulator is deliberately simple: two actuators on a rail, because the rover already supplies the fore/aft motion. That simplicity makes the whole thing solvable in closed form - the reach, the precision, and the configurations to avoid. Here it is, worked, then made draggable.

Governing relation
\[ L_i = \lVert G - B_i \rVert \]
Each actuator length is a distance in closed form - and every safe pose keeps the transmission angle in [45 deg, 135 deg].
Full derivation / from target point to safe configuration

How the arm is solved

Because the arm has only two actuators, the kinematics are closed-form - no iterative solver, cheap enough for the edge (DD-47). We follow Module 8 Sec. 7 exactly.

Two actuators on a rail make the inverse kinematics a closed-form distance.
\[ L_i = \lVert G - B_i \rVert = \sqrt{(y-B_{iy})^{2} + (z-B_{iz})^{2}} \]

For a target gripper point \(G = (y, z)\) and fixed actuator anchors \(B_1, B_2\) on the rail, each actuator length is simply the distance. If either length falls outside the actuator stroke \([L_{\min}, L_{\max}]\), the target is out of reach - the forward two-circle intersection has no valid solution.

Consequence

No iterative solver is needed - the pose is exact and cheap enough to run on the edge (DD-47).

The reachable span must cover the bed, so workspace couples to track width.
\[ \mathrm{span}(w, L_{\min}, L_{\max}) \;\ge\; W_{\mathrm{bed}} + \text{margin} = 1.7\ \mathrm{m} \]

Because the rover supplies the fore/aft motion, the arm only has to span the bed transversely. The reachable transverse span - set by the anchor separation \(w\) and the stroke \([L_{\min}, L_{\max}]\) - must exceed the bed width plus margin.

Consequence

The workspace couples to track width: a system-level constraint tying the arm to the rover geometry, not a local arm property.

Singularities are geometric - and the design enforces a hard keep-out.
\[ \det J \sim \sin\gamma \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \gamma \to 0^\circ \text{ or } 180^\circ \text{ is } \textsf{singular} \]

The Jacobian \(J\) maps actuator rates to gripper motion. A singularity is where \(\det J = 0\) - the actuator lines become geometrically degenerate and force transmission collapses. The transmission angle \(\gamma\) between the two actuators captures it, and the scalar distance from a singularity is the manipulability:

\[ \mu = \sqrt{\det\!\left(J J^{\mathsf{T}}\right)} \]

Precision degrades near singularities too: joint error scales into task error through \(J\), so error blows up as \(\mu \to 0\). The design therefore keeps \(\gamma \in [45^\circ, 135^\circ]\) and \(\mu \ge \mu_{\min}\) everywhere the arm works (SR-C-07, DD-44).

Consequence

A quantitative keep-out guarantees controllable, safe force transmission - and the accuracy that comes with staying clear of \(\mu \to 0\).

Engineering Insight EI-08 / the design behind the simplicity

The cheapest axis is the one you never build

EI-08 - Design the Degree of Freedom Out

Before adding an axis to solve a problem, ask whether the architecture can remove the need for it. This entire map is analyzable in closed form - reach, precision, singularities, all exact - only because the arm has just two actuators. Letting the drive-over rover supply the fore/aft motion removed a whole arm axis: the most reliable axis on the machine, because it does not exist - nothing to build, calibrate, break, or drive into a singularity. The cost is real but paid elsewhere: the workspace now couples to track width (this map) and the timing to the drive (CEC-01). The discipline is to move complexity to where it is cheapest to bear, not to pretend it vanished.

Engineering takeaway / what to do next

Where the next effort belongs

  • -> The safe region shrinks toward the edges of the bed, where \(\gamma\) approaches the keep-out. If any fruit sits in the near-singular band, do not fix it with a better controller - the geometry is the problem.
  • -> The highest-leverage move is anchor placement and stroke - where \(B_1, B_2\) sit and how far the actuators travel. Re-siting the anchors reshapes the entire safe region for free, before any control effort.
  • -> Decision: the next effort belongs in workspace geometry - anchors, stroke, and staging position - not in controller tuning. Precision degrades fastest near singularities, so keeping the corridor in the safe region buys accuracy that no gain schedule can.
