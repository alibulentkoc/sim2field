Solution 4 - Why the dominant term wins
ddσi ⁣[ ∑jσj2]=ddσi ⁣[ σi2]=2σi\frac{d}{d\sigma_i}\!\left[\, \sum_j \sigma_j^{2} \right] = \frac{d}{d\sigma_i}\!\left[\, \sigma_i^{2} \right] = 2\sigma_idσi​d​[j∑​σj2​]=dσi​d​[σi2​]=2σi​

The marginal reduction in the variance sum is 2σi2\sigma_i2σi​ - directly proportional to the source's current size. So the largest source has the steepest slope and yields the biggest return per unit reduction. The ranking can change after each cut: once you shrink the former dominant term, a different source may now be largest, so you re-rank and repeat.

Solution 5 - Defend a design choice (model answer)

A wider stereo baseline improves depth precision (lowers σZ\sigma_ZσZ​) but enlarges the sensor head and can push the cameras past the frame width, and a closer standoff improves depth resolution but shrinks the field of view, so fewer fruit are seen per frame - which pressures the cycle-time budget (CEC-01) by forcing more, slower passes. A reasonable recommendation: pull the baseline lever first, because depth precision improves roughly with baseline and it does not sacrifice field of view; treat standoff as a fallback only if packaging allows. (Any answer that names a concrete conflict and justifies a lever is acceptable.)
