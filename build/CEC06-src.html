Foundational Concept / CEC-06 / Module 13 / The Fidelity Ladder

A simulation is only evidence where it has been validated.

Simulation-first development is fast and cheap - and dangerous, because a convincing simulation can be confidently wrong. What makes it rigorous rather than merely simulated is a ladder: match the fidelity of the model to the decision it must inform, and trust each rung only after validating it against reality. Here is that ladder, and the rule that governs when a result counts as proof.

Governing relation
\[ \text{admissible} \iff \bigl(r_{\mathrm{ev}} \ge r_{\mathrm{req}}\bigr) \;\wedge\; \mathrm{validated}(r_{\mathrm{ev}}) \]
A claim is evidence only when its rung is high enough for the decision - and has been validated against reality.
The ladder - matching fidelity to the decision

Six rungs, each earning its claims

Fidelity is not free, and more is not always better. Each rung of the ladder can support only certain claims; climbing higher costs effort but unlocks harder questions. The discipline (Module 13, Doc G) is to spend fidelity where the decision is sensitive to it - no lower, no higher.

The rule that governs admissible evidence

Match the rung to the decision's sensitivity - no lower, no higher.

A reach question is purely geometric - the lowest rung answers it. A grasp-will-not-bruise question depends on contact mechanics that only appear high on the ladder. Using a low rung for a fidelity-sensitive decision is over-trust; using a high rung for an insensitive one wastes effort.

Consequence

Spend fidelity where the decision is sensitive to it - the binding rung, not the prettiest one.

A rung is admissible evidence only after it is validated against reality.
\[ \mathrm{admissible}(\text{claim}) = \bigl(r_{\mathrm{ev}} \ge r_{\mathrm{req}}\bigr) \;\wedge\; \mathrm{validated}(r_{\mathrm{ev}}) \]

A rung is admissible evidence only after it has been checked against reality to a stated tolerance (Doc G per-rung acceptance gates). An unvalidated rung - however detailed the simulation looks - is not evidence yet.

Consequence

The ladder turns "the simulation says so" into "a validated rung says so" - a different, and much stronger, claim.

The final, fidelity-sensitive claims - grasp-will-not-bruise, perception recall - are field-mandatory: interim sim rungs (R3, R4) give evidence, but the ground-truth claim rides R6, the real machine in the real field.

Consequence

The concept - rigour, not just simulation

What makes simulation-first trustworthy

CEC-06 - The Simulation-First Fidelity Ladder

Match the fidelity of a model to the decision it must inform, find where fidelity is binding, and validate each rung against reality before trusting it. This is the tool that makes a simulation-first program rigorous rather than merely simulated. A twin that designs, trains, and verifies the machine is only allowed to serve as evidence - for V&V, the safety case, the deployment argument - on rungs that have been validated to a stated tolerance. The failure it guards against is seductive: a detailed, beautiful simulation that has never been checked against a real melon, cited as proof that the grasp will not bruise. The ladder turns "the simulation says so" into "a validated rung says so," which is a different and much stronger claim.

Engineering takeaway - what to do next

How to use the ladder

  • -> For every claim, ask two questions before citing a simulation: is this rung high enough for the decision, and has it been validated? If either answer is no, the result is not yet evidence.
  • -> Spend fidelity effort where the decision is sensitive to it. The contact/fluid rung (R3) is the binding one for grasp-will-not-bruise - validating it against real fruit is the highest-leverage twin work, and it is the same measurement Doc C and Doc E depend on.
  • -> Do not over-build low-stakes rungs: a reach question is settled at R1, and a photoreal contact simulation for it wastes effort that the binding rung needs.
  • -> Decision: treat the fidelity-sensitive, field-mandatory claims (grasp-bruise, perception recall) as on the critical path - they ride R6, so the pilot is not optional. Plan the validation campaign that climbs the ladder to them deliberately.
