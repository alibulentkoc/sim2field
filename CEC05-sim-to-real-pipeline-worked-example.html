A policy that overfits the simulator looks perfect - right up until the field.

Grasping cannot be learned on real fruit at scale - too slow, too destructive. So the policy is trained in the twin, which supplies massive, cheap, safe experience. The danger is that it learns the simulator's exact physics instead of the task, and fails on the first real melon. The pipeline that prevents this has one discipline: randomize the conditions the learner must survive, then validate on real data before trusting the transfer.

Full derivation - why randomization transfers

The reality gap, and how randomization closes it

Domain randomization is robust optimization: train for expected return across a distribution of conditions.
π∗=arg⁡max⁡π Eξ∼p(ξ) ⁣[ Eπ ⁣[ G∣ξ ]]\pi^{\ast} = \arg\max_{\pi}\ \mathbb{E}_{\xi \sim p(\xi)}\!\left[\, \mathbb{E}_{\pi}\!\left[\, G \mid \xi \,\right] \right]π∗=argπmax​ Eξ∼p(ξ)​[Eπ​[G∣ξ]]

Let the simulator's uncertain parameters ξ\xiξ - contact stiffness, friction, mass, sensor noise, lighting - be drawn from a randomization distribution p(ξ)p(\xi)p(ξ). Train the policy to maximize expected return across that whole distribution, not one fixed setting. A policy optimal in expectation across a broad p(ξ)p(\xi)p(ξ) is robust to the specific real ξ\xiξ it later meets.

Consequence

That is the mathematical statement of why randomization transfers - robustness by construction, not by luck.

The reality gap is a distribution shift between training and field.

The reality gap is the mismatch between the training distribution p(ξ)p(\xi)p(ξ) and the field's true conditions. If the field lands inside what you trained on, the policy has seen its like and performs; if it lands outside, the policy is extrapolating - and a simulator-overfit policy falls off a cliff. The gap is measured by comparing real performance to sim performance, and closed by widening or retuning p(ξ)p(\xi)p(ξ).

Consequence

The gap is a measurable quantity, not a vibe - and it is invisible until you compare against real data.

Robustness trades against optimality - so randomize just wide enough.

Widening p(ξ)p(\xi)p(ξ) buys robustness but costs peak performance: a policy that must handle a huge range of conditions cannot be perfectly tuned to any one. Too narrow and it overfits the sim; too wide and it is mediocre everywhere. The craft is to randomize just wide enough to cover the field's plausible range.

Consequence

Real-data validation confirms the choice - the acceptance gate that converts "it works in sim" into evidence.

The concept - transfer is earned, never assumed

What makes learned behavior deployable

CEC-05 - The Sim-to-Real Pipeline

Develop learned behaviors in simulation under deliberately randomized conditions, then transfer to the field, measuring and closing the reality gap with real-data validation as an acceptance gate. The twin gives massive, safe, cheap experience that real fruit never could - but a policy that overfits the simulator is worse than useless, because it looks excellent in the only place you have been looking. Domain randomization forces the learner to be robust rather than tuned; validation on real data is what converts "it works in sim" into evidence. This is the operational bridge across the development/runtime boundary (CEC-04): learning happens on the development side, but transfer must be earned and measured, never assumed.

Engineering takeaway - what to do next

How to run the pipeline

  • -> Never trust an in-sim performance number on its own. It is measured in the one place the policy might be overfit to - the reality gap is invisible until you validate on real data, so the validation gate is not optional.
  • -> Randomize just wide enough to cover the field's plausible conditions. Too narrow overfits the simulator (a cliff when the field drifts); too wide throws away peak performance. Widen p(ξ)p(\xi)p(ξ) to close a measured gap, not preemptively.
  • -> Identify which parameters the field actually varies - contact stiffness, fruit mass, lighting - and randomize those. Randomizing parameters the field holds fixed only costs peak performance for no robustness gain.
  • -> Decision: gate deployment on a real-data validation campaign, and treat the grasp claim as field-mandatory (CEC-06 rung R6). Report the measured sim-to-field gap as the acceptance evidence - not the sim score alone.
