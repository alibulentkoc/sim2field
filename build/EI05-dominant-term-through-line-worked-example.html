Halving the dominant term produces a large drop in the total; halving a small term produces a negligible one. For the RSS (accuracy) budget the effect is even sharper: because the parts add as squares, a dominant term is squared before it is summed, so it dominates the total more strongly still.

Consequence

Effort spent anywhere but the dominant term is nearly wasted - improvements elsewhere are invisible until the dominant term comes down.

Engineering Insight EI-05 - the through-line of the course

The habit that generalizes

EI-05 - Attack the Dominant Error Source

In any additive budget, the largest term dominates the total - find it and reduce it first; effort spent shrinking small contributors is almost wasted. The math is unforgiving in a useful way: when one source dominates, the total is essentially that source, and improvements elsewhere are invisible until the dominant term comes down. It first appeared as the placement error budget (CEC-03), but it is not about accuracy - it is about budgets. The same move governs the power budget (which subsystem to make efficient), the reliability budget (which failure mode to harden), and the cost budget (which component to redesign for volume). Budget first, find the dominant term, spend effort there, then repeat until no single term dominates. That discipline - not any one equation - is what the course is really teaching.

Engineering takeaway - what to do next

How to use this on any budget

  • -> Before optimizing anything, write the budget down and rank the terms. The dominant term is usually not the one you understand best or can change most easily - measure, don't guess.
  • -> Spend effort on the dominant term until it is no longer dominant, then re-rank. Improving a small term to the third decimal while the dominant term is untouched is the classic wasted-effort trap.
  • -> Watch for the fixed-but-largest case: in the cost budget the platform is the biggest line but is externally fixed, so the dominant attackable term is the manipulator. Attack the biggest term you can actually move.
  • -> Decision: treat accuracy, energy, reliability, and cost as the same problem - an additive budget with a dominant term - and one habit handles all four. The leverage is real: here, aiming at the dominant term beats a small term by 5x to 15x for comparable effort.
