Worked Example / Module 6 / CEC-04 / The No-Cloud Edge Boundary

The cloud isn't slow. It's unbounded - and that's worse.

A harvester's control loop must close in a guaranteed 20 milliseconds, worst case. Not on average - worst case, every time, or the grasp acts on stale information. That single requirement decides where the computer lives. Here is the latency budget, worked on the target device, and the reason the cloud is on the wrong side of a hard line.

How the budget is closed

Perception and control share a slice of the cycle time (CEC-01). The compute job is to fit every stage inside it - measured on the actual device, not estimated. We follow Module 6 Sec. 7 exactly.

Sum the per-stage latencies and hold them under the compute share of the cycle.

The ratified budgets (Doc B Sec. 5.5): end-to-end sense-to-act ≤300 ms\le 300\ \mathrm{ms}≤300 ms, and the inner control loop ≤20 ms\le 20\ \mathrm{ms}≤20 ms worst-case. Populate the stages with measured latencies on the target device (EI-06), find the dominant stage (EI-05), optimize it, and re-measure.

Consequence

The budget is closed on the device, stage by stage - an average that fits is not a budget that closes.

Detection dominates - and its latency is a precision-allocation choice.

The neural-network detect stage dominates the budget. The levers to fit it: quantize the model (INT8 vs FP16 vs FP32 - trading a little recall for large latency and power savings, EI-04), move to a faster accelerator, reduce input resolution, or - last resort - loosen the cycle-time budget by staging slower. Quantization is a precision-allocation problem with the same shape as the error budget (CEC-03): spend precision where it changes the outcome.

Consequence

Optimize the dominant stage first (EI-05), and cut latency, power, and heat together rather than chasing a cheap stage.

A network has no bounded worst case, so the cloud cannot be in the loop.
tcontrol≤20 ms (worst-case)buttRTTworst=unboundedt_{\mathrm{control}} \le 20\ \mathrm{ms}\ \text{(worst-case)} \qquad\text{but}\qquad t_{\mathrm{RTT}}^{\mathrm{worst}} = \text{unbounded}tcontrol​≤20 ms (worst-case)buttRTTworst​=unbounded

A cloud round-trip adds network latency that is variable and, at the tail, unbounded - no network guarantees a worst case. Even on a good link the round-trip alone dwarfs the 20 ms control deadline, and the worst case cannot be certified at all (SR-P-35 demands a bounded worst case).

Consequence

The perception-to-control path stays on the robot, with no cloud in the control loop (CEC-04). The cloud may still do offline work - training, fleet analytics - just never inside the real-time loop.

The budget, live - move the computer

Put the computer on the edge, or in the cloud

Switch the compute location and watch the latency budget. On the edge, tune the model precision to fit. In the cloud, watch the network round-trip blow the control deadline - and note the hatched tail: the worst case has no ceiling.

Engineering Insight EI-06 - measure on the target

The only latency that counts is on the device

EI-06 - Measure on the Target

Latency, power, and accuracy are properties of the model on the actual hardware - not of the model in the abstract. A network that is fast on a workstation can miss the budget on the edge device; a layer that looks cheap can be memory-bound and dominate. Measure each stage on the target, find the dominant one, optimize it, and re-measure - never optimize a stage you have not measured. The no-cloud boundary (CEC-04) is the same discipline taken to its conclusion: the target is the robot, and the only latency that counts is the one you can guarantee there.

Engineering takeaway - what to do next

Where the next effort belongs

  • -> Do not argue edge-vs-cloud on average latency. The control loop needs a bounded worst case (≤20 ms\le 20\ \mathrm{ms}≤20 ms), and a network has none - that alone settles it. Keep the perception-to-control path on the robot.
  • -> On the edge, spend effort on the dominant stage (detection) - quantize first (INT8 buys large latency and power savings for a little recall), and only reach for a bigger accelerator if that still misses the budget.
  • -> Watch the power ceiling, not just latency: FP32 may fit the time budget yet exceed the thermal/power budget (M12). Right-sizing the model cuts latency, power, and heat together.
  • -> Decision: keep the real-time loop on-device and use the cloud only for offline work (training, fleet analytics). Then tune precision on the target until the dominant stage fits, re-measuring after each change.
